## The Automation Readiness Framework

Not everything should be automated. Here's how to decide:

### Score Each Process (1-5 on each dimension)

| Dimension | Question |
|-----------|----------|
| **Frequency** | How often does this happen? (1=once/year, 5=multiple times/day) |
| **Complexity** | How many decision points? (1=single step, 5=complex branching) |
| **Error cost** | What happens if it's done wrong? (1=minor, 5=catastrophic) |
| **Time cost** | How many hours/week? (1=<1hr, 5=>10hrs) |
| **Documentation** | Is the process documented? (1=fully, 5=tribal knowledge) |

### The Automation Priority Matrix

- **Score 15-25**: Automate immediately. High frequency, low complexity, well-documented
- **Score 10-14**: Automate next quarter. Good ROI but needs some process work first
- **Score 5-9**: Keep manual for now. Too complex or infrequent to justify automation

### Quick Wins (Score 15+)

- Email routing and triage
- Report generation and distribution
- Customer onboarding sequences
- Invoice processing
- Content cross-posting
- Data entry and reconciliation
- Calendar scheduling
- Social media monitoring

## The Real Barrier Isn't Technology

Here's what I've learned after running an agent-operated company:

**The #1 barrier to automation is not knowing what to automate.**

Most founders think automation means "hire a developer to build a custom tool." That's expensive, slow, and often wrong.

The modern approach is different:

1. **Map your processes** — Write down every recurring task your team does
2. **Score them** — Use the framework above
3. **Start with the highest score** — Don't try to automate everything at once
4. **Use AI agents, not scripts** — Scripts break when things change. Agents adapt
5. **Measure the before/after** — Track hours saved, errors reduced, speed improved

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Let me give you a real example from our operations:

**Process**: Customer support triage

**Before**: Every support email went to a human. They read it, categorized it, routed it, and either responded or escalated. Average response time: 4 hours.

**After**: An AI agent reads every incoming email, categorizes it (billing, technical, feature request, bug), routes it to the right team member, and for common issues, drafts a response for human review. Average response time: 15 minutes.

**Time saved**: 25 hours/week
**Cost saved**: $2,400/month
**Customer satisfaction**: Up 34%

One process. One agent. $28,800/year saved.

Now multiply that across 10-15 processes in your company.
